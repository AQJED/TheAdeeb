Reinventing the Flat Tire: Why I Stopped Writing Math

The Assignment

As a software integration engineer, my job is often to act as the translator between two systems that refuse to speak the same language.

I was assigned to bridge a data source with a visualization engine. The requirement was simple. Take the coordinates from the first system and forward them to the second so we could visualize the movement in real time.

The Breakdown

I parsed the packet. I sent the coordinates. And the result was confusing.

First, the object appeared in the middle of the ocean, thousands of miles from where it was supposed to be.

Second, and perhaps more disturbing, the model was moving sideways. I would command a right turn and the visual would drift left while banking strangely. It turned out the visualization engine was using a counter clockwise positive system relative to the left axis, while the data source was using standard clockwise north up.

The Rabbit Hole

Instead of looking for a standard conversion library, I tried to become a geodesist.

I was convinced I just needed to fix the math. I opened a text editor and went down a Wikipedia hole. I was reading articles on oblate spheroids, WGS84 eccentricity, and quaternion rotation matrices.

"I wasted three days manually writing C++ trigonometry functions to convert coordinate systems and invert rotation matrices. I was reinventing a wheel that was invented 300 years ago, and I was making it square."

The Reality Check

My custom math was close enough for short distances, but as soon as the object moved far enough, the curvature of the Earth broke my linear approximations.

Frustrated, I finally did what I should have done on day one. I looked for a library.

I found an open source C++ library specifically designed for coordinate conversion. It handled the datum shift. It handled the rotation. I replaced three days of my spaghetti math with a single function call.

I compiled the code. The object snapped to the right location. It turned right when I said right.

The Takeaway

There is a pride in building it yourself. But in software integration, you are standing on the shoulders of giants.

Don't invent the wheel. Someone smarter has already written a library to make the wheel spin perfectly. Use it.
